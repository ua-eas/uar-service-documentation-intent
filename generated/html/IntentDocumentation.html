Preamble

These GitHub-hosted repositories hold canon copies of versioned documentation relevant to the planned upgrade and subsequent sustained operation of the University of Arizona’ s UAccess Research service. If you have any questions about the contents of this repository, please contact Mike Simpson .

There are two repositories: the first (“ uar-service-documentation” ) contains the actual documentation artifacts (spreadsheets, diagrams, project plans, etc.), and the second (“ uar-service-documentation-intent” ) contains contextual and explanatory notes that tie all of the individual documentation artifacts together into a (hopefully) cohesive whole.

All of this is managed from inside the Eclipse IDE, which is an admittedly unusual environment to use for either word processing or project management. But it has a familiar interface, tremendous flexibility and extensibility, and convenient integration with GitHub-based version control. It also has the Mylyn Intent plugin, which is what ties the two repositories (artifacts and explanation) together, using literate programming principles and techniques established by Donald Knuth at least as early as 1984.

If all of the above sounds overly ambitious and a little unfamiliar, ask Mike to give you the hard sell the next time you have an hour or so for soapboxing.

Project Baseline

I did the original baseline estimation and project planning using an Excel spreadsheet:

  • (Reference to platform:/resource/uar-service-documentation/Project Workbook/UAR Upgrade Project Baseline.xlsx)

The planning procedure was as follows:

  • I roughed out a per-module phased labor estimate (“ Per Module Labor” tab), which defines the 20-week delivery cycle needed to deliver a defined subset of functionality from business need to deployed-in-production: think of this as akin to a Scrum-style sprint, that takes a single module through requirements gathering, functional remediation (coding of fixes and/or enhancements), and knowledge management (data modeling and report generation). For each phase, I estimated FTE labor requirements across six labor categories, from business office to support staff.
  • I proposed five delivery cycles (three to duplicate existing functionality, plus two additional cycles to deploy new modules), and then I played with overlapping the cycles to a greater or lesser extent, watching what happened to the peak FTE counts for any given week of the project, and trying to find a happy medium between how long the project would take and what kind of head count we could reasonably expect to field.
  • I found an overlap that peaked business analysts and software developers at 3.0 FTE each, which fit our staffing constraints, if we were willing to shuffle some labor from UAccess Financials over to Research for the duration of the project.
  • That overlap produced a total of 44 weeks of active development, roughly 10 months on the calendar, with a total commit of 6.6 annualized FTE over that period.
  • All of the above is collated on the “ Project Baseline” tab at the front of the spreadsheet. Also on that tab is a “ Days of Work (DoW)” track that shows the intensity of labor per week in terms of how many total FTE days are dedicated in that week.
  • Note also that this baseline shows us completing the third delivery cycle in week 32 of the project; this is the point at which we could cut over production services to the new version, delivering the results of the last two cycles as regular production releases.
  • The “ Labor Commits” tab shows the labor commit per week per project role; basically it’ s just another view of how completely any indidividual person is going to be consumed by the project in that week.
  • The “ Modules By Release” tab just captures what functionality was delivered in what Kuali Foundation release, based on the release notes that accompanied each one: that information is a little scattered and sometimes contradictory, so I wanted to try and get it all down in one spot for ease of reference.

Service Model

The basic service model is drawn largely from the ITIL 2011 specifications, keeping most of the terms, but simplifying the names, modifying for local usage, and adapting them to be more open source-friendly. In ITIL, there are five main subdivisions of IT services: strategy, design, transition, operation, and improvement. For our purposes, we keep those five names, but we group three tightly-related stages (design, transition, and operation) into a supercategory and call it “ delivery” .

Strategy

The objective of service strategy is to assess strategic business needs, manage the service portfolio (implementing new services, decommissioning old ones), track and anticipate demand for services, and maintain good partner relationships. The outputs of the service strategy include new initiatives, programs that fall under those initiatives, projects within those programs, and implementation phases within those projects.

Processes

Portfolio

The purpose of the portfolio process is to initiate, manage, assess, and retire the various initiatives, programs, projects, and services that represent the sum of an organization’ s information technology capabilities. The promise of the portfolio process is that it will quantify traditionally-informal efforts, enabling measurement and comparative evaluation of the organization’ s activities.

Financial

The purpose of the financial process is to track, analyze, and balance the costs, quality, and risks associated with delivered services, to inform and enable decisions made about the assets and resources used to provide services. The promise of the financial process is that it will promote stewardship and detailed accountability for how services are planned, implemented, and delivered.

Demand

The purpose of the demand process is to manage and forecast current and future demand for the products and services delivered by the organization, and attempt to balance those demands with resource and scheduling constraints. The promise of the demand process is that it will create a rational, well-informed decision path that converts ideas and opportunities into approved organizational activities.

Relationship

The purpose of the relationship process is to provide a complete model of current business relationships and their value across time, and to make those relationships explicit and measurable. The promise of the relationship process is that it will enable stakeholders to develop, evaluate, and leverage the network of relationships within which the organization is embedded.

Outputs

Initiatives

An initiative represents a long-term organizational focus on a number of related programs and/or projects designed to be of benefit to institutional strategic objectives. An initiative may have an associated director and/or a steering committee, or similar structure.

Programs

A program represents a collection of two or more projects and other related activities, all sharing a common goal; an associated program manager will control dependencies and allocate resources across the projects within a program. Programs are designed to deliver outcomes and benefits related to the organization’ s strategic goals.

Projects

A project represents a temporary organization of resources, applied within a well-defined scope, which will deliver one or more outputs in accordance with a specific business case. Projects have clear beginning and ending dates, allocated budget, milestones, and deliverables. Each project will have a project manager responsible for it.

Phases

Projects will usually be decomposed into individual phases, with each phase associated with specific dates, resources, milestones, and deliverables. A phase may or may not have an associated phase manager.

Delivery

Once strategy determines demand, direction, and allocates available resources, delivery mechanisms engage to design, build, deploy, and maintain the requisite services and functions. Delivery is a continuous cycle (or pipeline) of work evolving steadily from business need to deployed functionality.

Design

The objective of service design is to ensure that service offerings meet appropriate availability, capacity, and continuity requirements, and can deliver requested functionality in a secure and reliable manner. The outputs of service design include both architectural/environmental tasks as well as requests for enhancement or fixes to service functionality.

Processes

Availability

The purpose of the availability process is to design, monitor, and deliver the agreed-upon amounts of service availability, in terms of reliability, resilence, maintainability and serviceability. The promise of the availability process is to guarantee that a given service matches or exceeds current and future needs in a cost-effective manner.

Capacity

The purpose of the capacity process is to design, monitor, and deliver the agreed-upon amounts of service capacity, in terms of performance and throughput. The promise of the capacity process is to supply enough capacity to meet current and future business demands, without requiring that the organization maintain excessive amounts of unused capacity.

Security

The purpose of the security process is to design, monitor, and deliver the agreed-upon amounts of service information security, in terms of confidentiality, integrity, and availability. The promise of the security process is to guarantee that service data is managed

Continuity

The purpose of the continuity process is to assess risk, evaluate recovery options, and plan and manage actual service recovery in the event of major outages or disasters. The promise of the continuity process is to maintain service continuity with minimal business impact across a disaster situation.

Functionality

The purpose of the functionality process is to define and manage the agreed-upon business functionality provided by the service. The promise of the functionality process is to ensure that what is delivered is what is needed, in terms of purpose and function.

Improvement

The objective of service improvement is to analyze current status and trending, identify opportunities for improvement, plan and implement organizational or process changes, and assess the positive or negative impacts of those changes. The outputs of service improvement include detailed metrics on current benchmark indicators, analyses of trending and possible improvements to be made, and defined action plans for implementing change.
